ICAR Integrated Pest Management (IPM) Guide for Indian Crops
Source: National Centre for Integrated Pest Management (NCIPM), ICAR, New Delhi

PRINCIPLES OF IPM
IPM combines multiple pest control tactics to keep pest populations below economic injury levels while minimizing risks to human health, beneficial organisms, and the environment.

HIERARCHY OF CONTROL METHODS (use in order):
1. Cultural control (crop rotation, resistant varieties, proper spacing)
2. Mechanical control (traps, hand picking, barriers)
3. Biological control (natural enemies, biopesticides)
4. Chemical control (last resort — use approved doses)

COMMON BIOPESTICIDES AND DOSES
Trichoderma viride: 4 g/kg for seed treatment; 2.5 kg/ha soil application in 250L water. Controls soil-borne pathogens (Fusarium, Rhizoctonia, Sclerotinia).
Beauveria bassiana: 1×10^8 cfu/mL @ 5 mL/L. Controls stem borer, leaf folder, aphids, whitefly.
Bacillus thuringiensis (Bt): 1 kg/ha. Controls caterpillars — bollworm, armyworm, diamond-back moth. Does NOT work on sucking pests.
Neem-based products (Azadirachtin 1500 ppm): 3–5 mL/L. Controls aphids, whitefly, thrips, mites. Also repels and disrupts feeding.
Verticillium lecanii: 1×10^6 cfu/mL @ 5 mL/L. Controls aphids, whitefly.

MONITORING AND ECONOMIC THRESHOLDS
Always scout fields before spraying. Chemical sprays should only be applied when pest populations cross the Economic Threshold Level (ETL).

Crops and ETLs:
- Rice stem borer: 5% dead hearts (vegetative) OR 2% white ears (reproductive)
- Rice BPH: 10 hoppers/hill
- Wheat aphid: 50 aphids/tiller
- Mustard aphid: 40–50 aphids/plant
- Cotton bollworm: 10 larvae/100 plants OR 8% boll damage
- Tomato fruitworm: 1 larva/plant OR 3% fruit damage

SUCKING PESTS — GENERAL APPROACH
Aphids, Whitefly, Thrips, Mites:
Step 1: Sticky traps (yellow for whitefly/aphids, blue for thrips) — 10–15 traps/ha for monitoring.
Step 2: Neem oil 5 mL/L + spreader at first appearance.
Step 3: Biopesticide (Verticillium, Beauveria) if infestation persists.
Step 4 (Chemical — only if Step 3 fails): Imidacloprid 70WG @ 35g/ha OR Thiamethoxam 25WG @ 80g/ha. Do NOT spray during flowering (kills bees).

LEAF-EATING CATERPILLARS
Includes: armyworm, semilooper, gram caterpillar, cabbage worm.
Step 1: Hand pick egg masses.
Step 2: Bt (Bacillus thuringiensis) @ 1 kg/ha spray.
Step 3: Chlorpyrifos 20EC @ 2.5 L/ha (only if Bt fails AND crop is not near harvest).
Waiting period: Do not use organophosphates within 14–21 days of harvest.

FUNGAL DISEASES — GENERAL APPROACH
Powdery mildew: Sulphur 80WP @ 3g/L OR Hexaconazole 5SC @ 1 mL/L.
Downy mildew: Metalaxyl-Mancozeb 72WP @ 2.5g/L.
Rust (all crops): Propiconazole 25EC @ 1 mL/L OR Tebuconazole 25EC @ 1 mL/L.
Wilt: No effective foliar spray. Use Trichoderma viride soil application 2.5 kg/ha.
Blight (early/late): Mancozeb 75WP @ 2.5g/L + Chlorothalonil 75WP @ 2g/L.

RESISTANCE MANAGEMENT
Never spray the same chemical class more than 2 consecutive applications.
Rotate between FRAC groups:
- DMI fungicides (propiconazole, tebuconazole) → Strobilurin → Carboxamide → back to DMI.
If one pesticide is not controlling the pest after 2 applications, switch to a different mode of action.

PESTICIDE SAFETY RULES (INDIA)
Only use pesticides registered under Insecticides Act 1968 and approved by CIB&RC.
Always read label before mixing — use correct dose.
Wear PPE: gloves, mask, goggles, rubber boots during spraying.
Re-entry period: Typically 24–48 hours after spraying.
Pre-harvest interval (PHI): Do not harvest crops within the specified days after last spray (label requirement).
Dispose empty containers by triple-rinsing, puncturing, returning to dealer.
Do NOT wash spray equipment near water bodies.

BENEFICIAL INSECTS TO PROTECT
Honey bees: Never spray insecticides during flowering.
Lady beetles (Coccinella septempunctata): Natural predators of aphids. Present in fields — do not spray if seen in abundance.
Lacewings (Chrysoperla): Larvae eat aphids, mites, whitefly eggs.
Parasitic wasps: Control caterpillar pests. Visible as small wasps.

COMMON MISTAKES FARMERS MAKE
1. Spraying on cloudy/rainy days — chemical washes off.
2. Using wrong nozzle — use flat-fan for broad coverage, hollow cone for dense canopy.
3. Spraying against wind — causes drift, human exposure.
4. Under-diluting (using less water) — uneven coverage.
5. Mixing incompatible chemicals.
6. Spraying during noon heat — chemical degrades fast.
Best spray time: Early morning (before 9 AM) or late afternoon (after 4 PM).
